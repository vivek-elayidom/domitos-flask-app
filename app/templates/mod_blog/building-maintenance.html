Building Maintenance

Building maintenance applies to all the activities required to maintain a working and liveable house. A single building requires a skilled team of multiple people in areas such as electrical , plumbing and HVAC, with different maintenance specialties. Without routine maintenance services, a building can easily become an unfriendly living and working place and thus maintenance technicians are invaluable. If you're looking to become a repair technician yourself one day or just being curious about what building maintenance is all about, we 're going to break down everything you need to know.

What is Building Maintenance?

Building maintenance encompasses a variety of tasks that help the tenants of a building create a safe and comfortable work or living environment. From anything inside a building, such as bathroom cleaning and the maintenance of electrical systems, to anything outside a building, such as lawn care and landscaping, routine maintenance services are performed to keep properties in tip-top form. Maintenance teams are usually split into various roles, depending on similar maintenance services. Usually this structure looks just like:

Janitorial : Typical daily tasks involve cleaning the house, mopping the floors, emptying the garbage, cleaning the bathrooms.

Maintenance engineer: Responsible for routine maintenance and repair of building equipment including HVAC, electrical and plumbing.

Maintenance Supervisor: Responsible for the timeline and delegation of tasks to be completed for overall maintenance of the building. Maintenance supervisors are also responsible for personnel management including recruitment and reviews.

Building maintenance requires a wide variety of activities depending on the company or organization in question. It involves a lot of "behind the scenes" work to keep a facility or building running and comfortable for its users. Maintenance of buildings involves cleaning common areas, periodically removing garbage and fixing damaged objects. It can include inspection, repair and maintenance of electrical systems, heating and air conditioning systems, as well as other utilities. House maintenance also applies in some cases to the outdoor properties and includes sprinkler control, lawn care, and landscape control.

Why Building Maintenance is Important?

Building maintenance is important as it ensures the safety and comfort of anyone staying in a building, whether they live or work there. From a business perspective, proper regular maintenance can save property owners money in the long run and contribute to a higher value of the property.

1- Keeps occupants safe

A poorly maintained building can pose hazards to those who work or live there. Constant maintenance helps to avoid dangerous conditions, whether it is peeling paint, an unstable walkway, or inadequate air circulation. To ensure this, security codes were created from organizations such as the Occupational Health and Safety Administration (OSHA) and the National Fire Protection Association (NFPA). Upholding these codes and completing ongoing general safekeeping procedures will avoid significant health hazards and unsafe work and living environments.

2- End up saving Money

Precaution is more cost-effective than a cure, which is another reason why maintenance services are critical for building. An immediate repair or replacement of a large network would be more expensive than ongoing routine maintenance. This is often easier to establish a budget for routine maintenance of building infrastructure than a repair or replacement that is unpredictable.

A maintenance service team can not only save money over time but also add to the overall value of the house. With a well-maintained, up-to-code building there's more interest that might mean more sales opportunities from selling, renting, or leasing spaces.

The objectives of building maintenance works are:

  1. Maintenance of equipment, structures and facilities in good working order
  2. Restoring it to original quality
  3. Upgrading the facilities according to the progress of the building technologies.


All the buildings deteriorate from the moment they are finished. Degradation intensity depends upon a variety of factors. The building's inhabitants can't control all the variables.

The following points shall be kept in mind during the design and development stages:



  1. Proper choice of materials for the building
  2. Choosing appropriate construction techniques
  3. Adequate construction and installation specifications
  4. Effective monitoring during construction and rectification of defects before final certification.
  5. Provision of sufficient space for properly designed landscaping

Depending on the nature of the construction, the building shall be sustained for an initial duration of 3 to 6 months immediately after the date of completion because there may be teething problems with any new building. If these are taken care of, the stress of maintenance will be reduced. The maintenance cost increases disproportionately to a higher level when there are any inherent defects in both design and construction and the expected building life is reduced. Building maintenance aims at an efficient and economical method of keeping the building and services fully usable. It requires various skills as affected by a building's occupancy and planned level of efficiency. Planning the works to be performed to keep the building in good condition requires high skills. Maintenance suggestions can also be a continuous method for optimizing the design and development phases.

Often, other company employees take for granted much of the work of the maintenance team. They simply expect the building to be kept clean, the snow to be removed in the winter and the air conditioning to be on in the summer heat. The people behind everything

A team of highly skilled maintenance technicians stands behind every properly maintained building. A successful technician in many fields including HVAC, electrical , and plumbing is well-trained, diligent, flexible, and detail-oriented.

In particular, the maintenance technicians:

  1. Inspect existing systems to actively look for issues
  2. Prioritize several projects and juggle projects
  3. Work to keep systems in the building "like new"
  4. Work typically a varied schedule of 40-60 hours per week
  5. Are frequently called upon for unforeseen or emergency repairs

Maintenance techs have many responsibilities and getting a skilled technician requires quality preparation. So if you're wondering how to become a technician in maintenance, here's what you need to know.


Organizations which use maintenance of buildings


Businesses: Construction maintenance services are required for just about every business. Smaller companies themselves tuck these duties into general management and outsource services. Larger enterprises have an in-house maintenance department to manage those needs.

Residential complexes: Apartment or condominium complexes maintain a team of construction maintenance workers to inspect, repair, and manage all of the complex's indoor and outdoor maintenance needs.

Government: Municipalities use construction maintenance teams to ensure that city houses, post offices, and libraries are in good working order to meet public needs.


What it takes to be a maintenance engineer

The path to becoming a maintenance engineer requires a combination of developing essential soft skills, expanding your technical knowledge with training and improving your resume through certifications. Deciding which of the different types of maintenance areas you want to focus on can help guide you as you also continue to train and educate yourself.

Important skills

Any aspiring maintenance technician will, of course, undergo training to learn the trade, but they should also have the special soft skills needed for the job. Typically, these types of skills are learned through training, from as early as elementary to post-high.Any of the competences include:


  1. Beware of data

Besides having the basic soft skills needed for a career in maintenance services, certifications and continuing training will help improve the requisite technical skills.

Apprenticeship and certification

Most community colleges, vocational schools and businesses provide apprenticeships to begin obtaining the advanced training required to be a maintenance tech. These programs are usually classroom style combined with on-the-job training. You also have the option of obtaining certifications in desired maintenance areas, instead of taking on generic maintenance responsibilities apprenticeships. Here are only a few certifications required for those seeking a maintenance career:

Certified Maintenance and Reliability Technician: This is an entry-level qualification, ideal for breaking into the maintenance engineering industry. The CMRP evaluation test is both predictive / preventative maintenance-identifying problems before they occur-and corrective maintenance-solving problems as they arise.

Building Systems Maintenance Certification: The International Building Owners and Managers Institute (BOMI) grants this certification. It covers both plumbing and HVAC systems maintenance, with a focus on environmentally friendly practices and energy efficiency management.

Building Operator Certification (BOC): This is a multi-level qualification with tests aimed at facilities personnel and maintenance engineers in all specialties. BOC's main values include the value of being a well-rounded technician with competency in various systems.

HVACR certification: This certification focuses specifically on HVACR systems for heating , ventilation , air conditioning and radiation. It also includes identifying Green alternatives and issuing initiatives to treat water. It is administered through the Home Builders Institute and Home Builders National Association, and covers both installation and repair of the system.

If maintenance does not become a priority, our building teams will get overworked with reactive tasks, money will be lost to decreased energy efficiency and premature replacement of building assets. We have the ability to manage maintenance as building managers in order to optimize the value of our facilities and increase company revenues. Enjoying occupants with well-managed buildings and prioritizing proactive construction management can actually pay dividends in our ability to avoid expensive repairs. Use the following three strategies to optimize your organisation's building management and reduce maintenance costs.

The Risks Of Poor Building Maintenance

Weak or inadequate management of the buildings will in many ways create risks for providers of early years. Poorly maintained buildings can be dangerous and inherently unhealthy, particularly for small children. Problems such as humidity and cold caused by drafts and inefficient heating systems can cause accidents and injuries to children and neglected safety features, such as tendency to slip and travel hazards or electrical safety. Furthermore, the neglect of items like windows and doors can increase the risk of security breaches. Services such as electrical and water systems present many hazards if not maintained properly. Poorly maintained electrical systems can increase the risk of fires and electrocution and poorly maintained water systems can give rise to problems.

Bad maintenance of buildings, however, not only affects health but can also add a range of costs on an organization.

Preservation of the premises is an important factor in the efficient and cost-effective running of the buildings. Faults in a building's fabric, for example, particularly in older buildings, can worsen quickly and cause the building to deteriorate. Leaks and humidity are well known and evident defects which can cause long-term damage. Faults like dripping taps, ineffective insulation, cracked windows, unserviced boilers, broken radiator thermostats, missing roof slates and leaky gutters can all also lead to costly repairs. In addition to the building fabric, services such as water, lighting, heating and air-conditioning systems also need to be maintained to ensure the building is safe and user-friendly, and to ensure that energy is not wasted. In this context there is a direct link between poor maintenance and higher energy costs. Poorly maintained and poorly insulated buildings are difficult to keep warm, and the cost of heating can spiral.
